OpenAl automates software development?

Following GPT3, OpenAI launched another exciting/scary/thought-provoking ML project - a tool that writes software code in response to natural language input. You can make an arcade game entirely by telling the system how it should work and letting it write the code.

How far can this be generalised? Like GPT3 (of which this is a more focused version), it gives amazing demos and points to huge potential, in theory. ML scientists are impressed. But on the other hand, it’s not really a step towards HAL9000 - it has no understanding, context or judgment. This is still automation - machine learning gives you infinite interns, not super-humans. But the wave of AI that began in 2013 or so is perhaps now in a second stage. The first wave was ‘OMG it works now, and we can finally do image recognition or translation’, but the second is to see how much further we can push this. annoucement, demo

Apple's privacy stumble

After the backlash to its CSAM scanning announcement last week, Apple has mounted a big PR push, with interviews, more technical details and some tweaks (for example, CSAM scanning will only match images nominated by law enforcement in more than one country). Those might address technical concerns, and some of the confusion (mixing up 'iMessage will check kids' messages for nudes' with 'your iPhone will scan images you upload to iCloud for known CSAM'). But they don't really get to the heart of the philosophical objection: scanning on the cloud is limited to the data I upload, but scanning on the device could, in future, be changed to look at all of my data. This conflicts fundamentally with Apple's general theory of privacy - that anything that happens on the device is private and anything that happens in the cloud isn't. Here it's the other way around. interview, faq, security model

Google and Facebook moves on Child safety

Google and Apple both announced product and policy moves around child safety, controlling what ads they can be shown and creating mechanisms to block abusive behaviour. When people in crypto talk about building new social networks with decentralised, deterministic models, I wonder how such systems could make decisions like this. google, facebook

Facebook jumps on ad privacy

Facebook has made perhaps its first proactive move in the debate about tracking. With Apple, Google and regulators cutting off the exchange of targeting data between sites, Facebook now proposes a system for encrypted, anonymised exchange of data for tracking and measurement between FB and advertisers, and much more use of on-device learning, with the Facebook app analysing your interests without that necessarily being passed to the cloud. As for FLoC (below), this is all very early and depends a lot on industry consensus, but it also all feels like the 'bargaining' stage of grief rather than the 'acceptance' phase. announcement, interview

Google FLoC in question?

Google's FLoC was supposed to be the post-cookie privacy-first future of relevant ads, with your browser working out what ads you should be seeing and keeping the underlying data private. But there's been a lot of pushback both on competition grounds (should that be Google's decision?) and on arguments it's still not private enough. Now Google may be shifting to a much less granular (and less useful?) targeting model. Ultimately, we still don't have any clarity on how online ads will work once cookies go away. link

Wendy’s piles into ghost kitchens

US burger chain Wendy's is opening 700 new ghost kitchens, in partnership with the tech provider Reef. Almost half of US restaurant spending was already 'off-prem' before the pandemic (but mostly take-out or phone-based instead of app-based), and that's all getting reconfigured: if you don't need retail space, what does your cost and throughput look like? link

Google is proposing to pay people less if they move out of expensive cities to work from home permanently.
